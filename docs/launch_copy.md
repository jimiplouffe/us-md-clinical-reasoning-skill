## LinkedIn version

I open-sourced a compact U.S. clinical-reasoning skill for AI agents.

The point is not to make an “AI doctor.” That framing is dangerous and usually wrong.

The better use case is a safety and preparation layer:

- identify red flags before answering
- route urgent symptoms to the right level of care
- handle medication questions conservatively
- explain labs/reports without false certainty
- help patients prepare better questions for a clinician or pharmacist
- refuse fake notes, prescribing, overdose math, and unsafe shortcuts

The skill is structured around U.S. clinical conventions and source hierarchy: FDA, CDC/ACIP, USPSTF, NIH/NLM, AHRQ, and major specialty societies.

Open source because this kind of safety scaffolding should be visible, testable, and improvable.

Not medical advice. Not a medical device. Not a substitute for care. Just a practical guardrail for AI systems that will inevitably be asked health questions.

## GitHub release notes

First public release of a compact U.S.-based clinical-reasoning skill for AI agents.

Includes:

- triage-first operating model
- emergency escalation rules
- medication-safety module
- poisoning and mental-health crisis routing
- lab/report explanation format
- preventive-care source hierarchy
- hard boundaries for unsafe requests
- 10-case smoke-test suite
- safety policy and contribution guidance

This is not a clinician, medical device, diagnosis engine, or prescribing system. It is a prompt-level safety and reasoning layer for intake, education, and clinician-visit preparation.
